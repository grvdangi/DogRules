Vaccination

Puppies receive their first vaccine at 6-8 weeks of age. This vaccine is boostered every 4 weeks until the puppy is 16 weeks of age. Some vaccines ( Distemper, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza , Hepatitis and Rabies) become every 3 years while other vaccines (Lyme, Bordatella, Leptospirosis) are yearly.An annual examination is also performed. A year between physical examinations for your dog is like seven years between annual examinations for us.We recommend an annual physical examination whether your dog is receiving vaccinations or not.What are the Vaccinations?

 CoreVaccines:

these vaccines are recommended for all dogs regardless of their lifestyle. Distemper, Parvovirus, Parainfluenza and Hepatitis are combined into one for fewer injections and is referred to as DA2P+CPV.

1) Canine Distemper is an infectious disease and can be fatal in dogs. It affects the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and nervous systems and can cause seizures. Its highly contagious between infected animals. Other susceptible species are coyote, wolf, fox, ferret, mink, skunk, raccoon and some Large cats ( ie Lion)

2) Parvovirus causes severe vomiting and hemorrhagic diarrhea and can be fatal. This virus can live up to 7 months in the environment, thus posing a risk to many dogs. Unfortunately, this is still a common disease so vaccinating against parvovirus is very important.

3) Canine Infectious Hepatitis (Adenovirus) can cause problems with the liver, respiratory system, and eyes and can be fatal.

4) Parainfluenza causes an upper respiratory infection and is one of the viruses involved in kennel cough. It is highly transmissible between dogs.

5) Rabies is a virus transmitted from the bite of infected animals. All mammals, including humans can be infected and the disease is fatal. Fortunately, rabies in Nova Scotia is very rare. The last known case was of a cat in HRM in February 2003.

 NonCoreVaccines:

are vaccines that are recommended for some dogs depending on their lifestyle or when a dog come into the attack of a particular deseases

1) Bordatella Bronchiseptica is one of the bacteria that cause an upper respiratory infection in dogs, known as “kennel cough”. It is highly transmissible between dogs, and the vaccine is recommended for dogs that meet other dogs or travel to areas other dogs have been. (dog parks, kennels, groomers, obedience classes, etc.)

2) Lyme Disease is a caused by bacteria called Borrelia Burgdorferi transmitted from a tick bite. If infected with the bacteria, some dogs can develop problems with their joints, kidneys, or heart. This vaccine is recommended for dogs at higher risk of coming into contact with ticks (dogs that spend a lot of time in the woods, tall grass, or higher risk regions such as the South Shore).

3) Leptospirosis is a serious bacterial infection that can cause disease in both animals and humans. Dogs are at risk for contracting the bacteria when drinking out of puddles or ponds contaminated with urine from infected wildlife. Leptospirosis causes flu like symptoms at first and because of this, can be difficult to diagnose. It can progress to liver and kidney failure.

Vacination Schedule

  • 6-8 weeks: Puppy goes to new home. Examination, 1st set of vaccines, and deworming. Work on socialization, housetraining, crate training, getting familiar with family members and the routine at home (i.e. where puppy eats, drinks, leeps, etc.)

  • 12 weeks:Examination, 2nd set of vaccinations and deworming. Start socialization and obedience. Puppy is now safe to take in doggy areas (e.g. parks).

  • 16 weeks:Examination, last set of puppy vaccinations and de worming. Continue with training, obedience, and socialization

  • 6 Months:Puppy can be neutered or spayed.

  • 1 year, 4 months: Due for 1st yearly examination and vaccines. This is done at 1 year and 4 months of age because this is one year from his last set of vaccinations.
